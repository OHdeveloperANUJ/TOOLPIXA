# CGPA vs Percentage: The Complete Guide Nobody Gave You Before You Needed It

Four years of engineering, or three years of a commerce degree, and somewhere in the middle of all of it, you realise that nobody actually sat you down and explained how the grading system you're living inside works — what it means to the world outside your college, how different systems compare to each other, and whether the number your university prints on your marksheet is the one that actually decides anything about your future.

Instead, you picked it up in fragments. From a senior who said "anything below 7 and campus placements are over for you." From a friend who got rejected from a master's programme abroad because their 74% looked worse than someone else's 7.8 CGPA even though they were, arguably, the better student. From a job application that asked for your percentage and you had no idea what to type, because your university never gave you one.

This piece is the guide that should have been handed to you on day one. We're going to cover what CGPA and percentage actually are, how conversions work and why they're messier than they look, what actually matters for placements, higher studies, government jobs, and life after your first job — and most importantly, where people waste energy obsessing over a number that stopped mattering long before they stopped worrying about it.

## The Basics: What Each One Actually Measures

**Percentage** is the older system, and conceptually the simpler one. Your marks in each paper are out of 100, you add them up, divide by the number of papers, and you get a number between 0 and 100. It's transparent, intuitive, and directly comparable — a 72% from your college means roughly the same thing structurally as a 72% from another college running the same system, even if the actual paper difficulty varies (and it does, enormously).

**CGPA — Cumulative Grade Point Average** — is the system most Indian universities shifted toward over the last fifteen years, following a UGC (University Grants Commission) recommendation. Instead of raw marks, each subject gets a grade (O, A+, A, B+, B, and so on), and each grade corresponds to a grade point (typically on a 0-10 scale). Your CGPA is then the weighted average of all your grade points, weighted by the credits assigned to each course — a 4-credit subject counts more than a 2-credit elective when your average is being calculated.

The appeal of CGPA, at least in theory, is that it's more nuanced than a flat percentage. By weighting subjects by credit hours, it at least acknowledges that a 5-credit core course should count for more in your overall academic standing than a 1-credit mandatory library orientation class. It also, in principle, makes grade boundaries feel less arbitrary — the difference between 59 and 60 in a percentage system can flip you from one class to another, while in a grade-point system, both might comfortably sit in the same grade band.

In practice, the implementation in India has been so inconsistent across universities that the theoretical advantages get eaten up almost entirely by the confusion of comparing apples to entirely different species of apples.

## The Conversion Problem: Why "What's Your Percentage?" Is a Harder Question Than It Should Be

Here's where it gets properly messy, and this mess has real consequences — for job applications, postgraduate admissions, scholarship eligibility, and government job applications.

**The UGC's own formula** for converting CGPA to percentage on a 10-point scale is: **Percentage = CGPA × 9.5.** So a 7.5 CGPA on a 10-point scale converts to 71.25%. An 8.0 becomes 76%. A 9.0 becomes 85.5%.

This is a reasonable, widely accepted formula — except that a large number of universities either don't follow it, have their own institutional conversion table, or don't officially provide a conversion at all, leaving students to figure it out themselves when a job application demands a percentage.

Here's a quick illustration of how different the numbers can look across systems:

| CGPA / Score | System | Approximate Percentage Equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| 9.0/10 | UGC formula (×9.5) | 85.5% |
| 8.0/10 | UGC formula (×9.5) | 76.0% |
| 7.0/10 | UGC formula (×9.5) | 66.5% |
| 3.7/4.0 | US 4-point scale | ~92% (roughly) |
| 3.0/4.0 | US 4-point scale | ~75% (roughly) |
| 6.5/10 | Anna University pre-2019 | ≈ 60-65% (depends on credit pattern) |

That last row is not a typo. Anna University, which governs engineering colleges across Tamil Nadu, used to operate on a 100-mark scale but reported in CGPA; before their grading revisions, their system produced numbers that looked compressed to outsiders unfamiliar with their scale. A student graduating from an Anna University affiliate with a CGPA that converts to a modest-looking percentage might have been solidly in the top third of their class. And their transcripts frequently had no single "percentage" figure printed on them at all — just the CGPA and a grade sheet, leaving students to answer the percentage question with a converted figure that many HR teams frankly didn't know how to verify.

Mumbai University, VTU (Visvesvaraya Technological University in Karnataka), JNTU in Telangana/AP, Delhi University — they've all had their own variations, their own grace-mark policies, their own credit weightings. The result is a country full of graduates holding marksheets in formats that don't translate directly into each other, trying to compete on a dimension that's defined differently depending on which 50 kilometres of geography they happened to study in.

**IITs and NITs** add another layer. IIT CGPA on a 10-point scale looks similar numerically to scores from other universities, but an 8.0 CGPA from IIT Bombay is a different academic achievement from an 8.0 CGPA from a random state engineering college — not because the IIT student is necessarily smarter, but because IIT's grading is substantially more compressed at the top. Most IIT departments don't give out a 10/10; a 9.5 is exceptional; a 7.5 is a solid, respectable performance. Meanwhile, at many private colleges, a 8.5 or 9.0 CGPA might represent marking that is — charitably — generous. Companies and foreign universities recruiting from both simultaneously have been aware of this for years, which is why they look at the institution name alongside the CGPA rather than treating the number in isolation.

## For Campus Placements: The Cutoff Game and What It Actually Means

If you're a student currently in college, or a recent graduate, this is probably the section you came here for.

The reality of how most campus recruiters — particularly large IT companies, consulting firms, and BFSI players — handle academic scores is both simpler and more arbitrary than people expect. Most companies that visit for campus placements set a **minimum CGPA or percentage cutoff** purely as a screening device to manage applicant volume, not because they've done a rigorous study showing that a 6.75 CGPA predicts job performance significantly differently from a 6.5 CGPA. Common cutoffs you'll see across company types:

- **Big IT companies (TCS, Infosys, Wipro, Cognizant, HCL):** Typically around 6.0-6.5 CGPA (or 60-65%) for entry-level IT roles. Sometimes lower for bulk hiring drives.
- **Product and mid-tier tech companies:** Often 7.0-7.5 CGPA, since they're more selective in batch size and can afford to screen harder.
- **Investment banks and consulting (bulge bracket, MBB):** 7.5+ CGPA is common, sometimes 8.0+, alongside aptitude tests and interviews that do far more of the actual screening work.
- **Core engineering roles (manufacturing, PSUs visiting campus):** Varies widely; many PSUs use CGPA or percentage as a tiebreaker after written tests.
- **Government bank POs and similar:** Typically a minimum 60% or equivalent for eligibility, with the actual selection driven by competitive exams rather than CGPA.

What this means practically: if your CGPA is above the cutoff, it's largely done its job for placement purposes — it got you into the room. Once you're actually sitting in the interview, nobody is awarding you extra points for a 9.2 over a 7.8. They're evaluating your problem-solving, your communication, your technical knowledge, and your fit. The CGPA is a gate, not a score.

The one scenario where a higher CGPA genuinely continues to matter throughout campus placement season, beyond just clearing the initial filter, is when a company is using academic scores as a tiebreaker between otherwise similar candidates during shortlisting rounds. But even there, this is usually a second-order factor — someone with exceptional communication and problem-solving at 7.5 will typically beat a quieter candidate at 9.0 in the rounds that actually count.

The corollary to all of this is something seniors rarely say out loud: **if you're below the cutoff, no amount of other preparation matters for that company's campus process.** This is the genuinely painful part of the system — not because the cutoff is a perfectly accurate measure of ability, but because it's a hard administrative line that most companies won't make exceptions to during campus recruitment, since making exceptions for one candidate opens the door for every other candidate to argue their case individually, which nobody has time for during a mass recruiting drive.

## For Higher Studies Abroad: Where CGPA Really Does Carry Weight

If domestic placements treat CGPA as a binary gate, graduate admissions — particularly for MS and MBA programmes at foreign universities — treat it more like an actual data point in a holistic review. The difference matters.

For **MS admissions in the US, Canada, UK, Europe, or Australia**, your undergraduate GPA or CGPA is typically one of the core numerical inputs the admissions committee reviews, alongside your GRE score (where required), statement of purpose, letters of recommendation, research experience, and work experience for later applicants. Unlike campus placements, there's no binary cutoff that automatically eliminates you — but there are competitive ranges that essentially achieve the same effect for most applicants.

For top-50 US programmes in engineering, computer science, and quantitative fields, competitive applicants typically hold the equivalent of at least a 3.5 on a 4.0 GPA scale, which translates roughly to 7.9-8.0/10 on the Indian 10-point CGPA system using the UGC conversion. This is, however, institution-aware — admissions committees at well-known universities have country and institution-specific context. An 8.5 from IIT Kharagpur and an 8.5 from a lesser-known private college are not read the same way; the reader knows the grade inflation situation at various types of Indian institutions, and calibrates accordingly. What this means for you is that an aspirational CGPA from a genuinely rigorous programme — even if it's a 7.8 rather than a 9.0 — is often more competitive than a perfect-looking number from a programme known for its lenient marking.

The conversion question also surfaces painfully here. Foreign universities are used to receiving Indian transcripts in varying formats, and most experienced admissions staff know how to read them. But it's still your responsibility to provide a clear, verified conversion, ideally backed by a statement from your university or the WES (World Education Services) evaluation service if the programme requires it. Using the UGC formula (×9.5) is generally acceptable where no official institutional conversion exists, but it's worth checking the specific programme's instructions and contacting the admissions office if your transcript format might cause confusion.

For **MBA admissions abroad**, GMAT or GRE score typically does far more work than your undergraduate GPA, which recedes in importance significantly — especially for applicants with substantial work experience, where your professional track record, promotions, leadership story, and essays dominate the evaluation. A low CGPA from a decade ago, well-explained and overcome by a strong professional record, is often a surmountable weakness for MBA applicants in ways it genuinely isn't for fresh MS applicants.

## For Government Jobs and PSUs: A Whole Different Formula Problem

If you're preparing for UPSC, PSC exams, or recruiting through the PSU route via GATE, there's an additional layer of nuance around academic scores.

**UPSC Civil Services** has no minimum educational percentage requirement — just that you hold a university degree. Your marks from graduation simply don't factor into the selection process at all; it's entirely exam-based from mains onward.

**PSU recruitment through GATE**, on the other hand, typically does have an educational eligibility criterion, usually phrased as a minimum 60% aggregate (or 6.5 CGPA on a 10-point scale, or 6.0 for SC/ST/PwD) in engineering. Many PSU job notifications now explicitly accept both CGPA and percentage, and some include the specific conversion formula they use — which isn't always the standard UGC ×9.5 formula, incidentally. BHEL, ONGC, NTPC, IOCL, and others have each had their own phrasing in their recruitment notifications over the years. If you're applying to a specific PSU, read the eligibility clause in their actual notification rather than assuming a conversion formula — it genuinely differs.

**State government jobs and competitive exams** are similarly diverse — some specify percentage, some specify CGPA, many are just looking for a pass in the relevant degree without caring much about the score beyond the minimum threshold.

## After Your First Job: The Number That Stops Mattering (and Exactly When)

Here's the honest answer that most career advice dances around: for the overwhelming majority of working professionals in India, CGPA becomes entirely irrelevant approximately six to twelve months after joining their first job.

The moment you have actual work experience on your resume — a project you delivered, a team you worked with, a technology you built something real with — that experience is what every subsequent employer is evaluating. The hiring manager considering you for your second job is not asking "but what was your CGPA?" They're asking what you built, what you managed, what measurable outcome you produced, and whether you can do something similar or more complex for them. Your 7.4 versus someone else's 8.1 from three years ago is not a data point they're using.

There are narrow exceptions to this. Some elite employers — investment banks, consulting firms, certain finance and strategy roles — do ask for academic scores even at the lateral-hiring stage, especially if you're within a few years of graduation and your scorecard is still recent enough to count as signal rather than old noise. A handful of company application portals still have a CGPA/percentage field that's mandatory to fill even for mid-level roles, which can cause momentary confusion for applicants who've long since stopped thinking about their undergraduate marks. But these are edge cases rather than the norm.

The deeper point is this: whatever energy you're spending worrying about your CGPA in your final year of college should be proportional to how close you are to needing it as an active filter — campus placements, immediate postgraduate applications, first-job eligibility. If that window has passed, redirect the energy entirely. Your skills, your network, and your actual work record are your resume now.

## The Grading System Mess: A State-by-State, University-by-University Reality

It's worth being specific about how genuinely fragmented this landscape is, because the "CGPA vs percentage" debate often assumes a single, uniform system that simply doesn't exist.

**Central universities and institutions under the UGC's direct ambit** (DU, JNU, BHU, Hyderabad Central University, and so on) have largely moved to a standardised 10-point grading scale following the UGC's 2012 and subsequent guidelines. The grades, grade points, and credit structure are reasonably consistent in principle, even if implementation details vary.

**IITs** use a 10-point scale but with their own grading philosophy — letter grades (AA, AB, BB, BC, CC, CD, DD, FR) mapping to points on a 0-10 scale, with a culture of relative grading in many departments (meaning your grade reflects how you performed *relative to your batch*, not just your absolute marks). This is meaningfully different from absolute grading, and it's part of why a 7.5 at IIT can represent different underlying performance from a 7.5 at an institution with a purely absolute, marks-based grading system.

**State technical universities** — Anna University, VTU, JNTU, RGPV, SPPU (Pune), and others — have each evolved their own systems, often reformed multiple times over the years as regulatory requirements changed. Students who graduated in different years from the same university sometimes have marks in genuinely different formats because the grading scheme changed mid-stream. Anna University's shift to an absolute grading system from a relative one, and subsequent refinements, has produced a situation where alumni from the same institution graduating in different years hold transcripts that are internally inconsistent.

**Private universities** (Manipal, BITS Pilani, SRM, VIT, Amity, and dozens of others) each have their own accreditation, their own grading conventions, and their own credit structures. Some run on percentage, some on CGPA, and some provide both. Their conversion policies for external use are often whatever the registrar's office decides to issue.

**Arts, commerce, and humanities colleges** — particularly those affiliated to state universities like Mumbai University, Calcutta University, or Madras University — historically ran on percentage systems and largely still do, with some institutions adding a CGPA column to the marksheet alongside the percentage in recent years.

This fragmentation is genuinely not your fault as a student navigating it, but it does mean you can't assume any single piece of advice about CGPA "translating" cleanly is accurate for your specific institution without checking. When in doubt — for a job application, an admission requirement, or a scholarship — your university's registrar's office is the authoritative source on what your marks mean and how they should be represented to the outside world.

## Common Mistakes and the Obsessions That Don't Serve You

**Spending your third year chasing a 9.0 instead of building a portfolio.** For students in technical fields especially, a side project that shows you can actually build something interesting, or an internship where you contributed to real code that shipped, often does more for your employability than moving your CGPA from 8.2 to 8.7 at the cost of every weekend for a year. Above the placement cutoff, your CGPA has a diminishing marginal return on your employment outcomes; your skills don't.

**Assuming a low CGPA ends your career.** It limits some early pathways — campus placements at companies with strict cutoffs, and immediate applications to top foreign universities. But off-campus applications, where you apply directly to companies rather than through campus processes, don't always require or even ask for CGPA. Government competitive exams are CGPA-blind. Entrepreneurship is CGPA-blind. And as established above, your first few years of work experience rapidly makes the academic record a historical footnote. A low CGPA is a setback in specific early contexts, not a life sentence.

**Treating every conversion formula as interchangeable.** If a job application, a foreign university, or a government recruitment notification specifies how they want you to convert or present your CGPA, follow that specification exactly. Don't substitute the UGC ×9.5 formula if the institution has its own. Don't round up. Don't present a "converted percentage" as if it's a university-certified number if it's a formula you applied yourself — say so clearly, or the verification step can trip you up.

**Ignoring arrears and backlogs in favour of obsessing over CGPA.** In some grading systems, particularly at engineering colleges, the damage from a failed subject (arrear or backlog) to your placement eligibility and foreign admissions prospects is disproportionately larger than the damage from having a 7.2 CGPA instead of an 8.0. Many companies explicitly screen for "no active arrears" or "no history of backlogs" as a separate criterion from minimum CGPA. Clearing all papers on the first attempt — even at a modest grade — is strategically more valuable for most recruitment contexts than maximising your CGPA while carrying an uncleared subject into later semesters.

**Not knowing what's on your own marksheet.** This sounds obvious, but a surprisingly large number of students approaching their final year have never actually read their own semester transcripts carefully enough to know exactly how their CGPA is calculated, what the credit weighting of different courses is, and whether their university's marks are presented in a form that will confuse or require explanation to an external reader. Know your own academic record specifically, because you'll need to explain it on demand.

## The Deeper Question Nobody Asks: What Does Your Score Actually Measure?

Here's the honest, somewhat uncomfortable truth that sits underneath all of this. CGPA and percentage both primarily measure **how well you performed on your university's specific assessment system** — which is a combination of how well you understood the material, how well you performed under exam conditions, how well you navigated your specific university's format and expectations, and a healthy dose of how your professors happened to grade.

This is not nothing. The discipline of showing up, preparing systematically for assessments, managing a deadline-heavy multi-subject workload across multiple years — these are real skills that correlate with genuine competence. There's a reason employers use academic scores at all, even crude ones: they're a cheap, available proxy for conscientiousness, which turns out to be one of the more stable predictors of professional performance.

But it's also not the whole picture, or even most of it. The student who graduated with a 7.0 CGPA because they were simultaneously building a startup, running the college fest, and taking on freelance work is not the same as the student who has a 7.0 CGPA because they simply didn't engage with their coursework. The student with a 9.0 who spent four years memorising model answers and has never built anything independently is not equivalent to the student with a 9.0 who also published a paper and built three real projects. The number alone doesn't carry this nuance, and the people evaluating you at any stage beyond the initial filter are well aware of this — which is exactly why the resume, the SOP, the interview, the portfolio, and the references exist: to carry the context that a number on a marksheet fundamentally cannot.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**My university gave me CGPA but the job application asks for percentage. What do I enter?**
Use your university's official conversion formula if one exists — check with your registrar's office or your university's official website. If no official formula is stated, the UGC's standard formula (CGPA × 9.5 for a 10-point scale) is widely accepted. Enter the converted figure and if there's a remarks or notes field, briefly state that it's a CGPA-to-percentage conversion using the UGC formula.

**Is a 7.5 CGPA good or bad?**
It entirely depends on context. For most campus placement processes at large IT companies, it's above the cutoff and does the job it needs to do. For top consulting and finance roles with 8.0+ cutoffs, it might screen you out. For most foreign MS applications outside the top-20 programmes, it's broadly competitive. For a GATE-based PSU application, it's typically above the 6.5 minimum threshold. There's no universal answer — the number is only meaningful relative to a specific context.

**Does CGPA matter for UPSC?**
No. UPSC Civil Services exams have no minimum percentage or CGPA requirement beyond holding a degree. The selection is entirely through the Prelims, Mains, and Interview stages, and your undergraduate marks are not used at any point in the evaluation.

**My CGPA dropped significantly in one semester due to health issues. Should I explain this in applications?**
Yes, for contexts where you have the opportunity to do so — a statement of purpose for foreign admissions, or an interview where it comes up naturally. Don't volunteer it unnecessarily on every application, but where an unexplained dip might look like lack of effort or engagement, providing brief context is better than leaving the reader to assume the worst.

**Can I improve my CGPA in the final year?**
To a limited extent. CGPA is a cumulative weighted average, so earlier semesters with poor performance are already baked into the number with full weight. A very strong final year can meaningfully improve your score if your earlier performance was consistently poor rather than concentrated in one bad semester — the math matters here. Calculate where you actually stand and what's achievable, rather than either giving up or assuming a dramatic recovery is possible.

**My college gives both CGPA and percentage on the marksheet. Which should I use?**
Use whichever the specific application or institution asks for. If both are genuinely certified on your official marksheet, either is valid and both are defensible — your marksheet is the primary document.

## The Honest Bottom Line

CGPA and percentage are measuring similar things in different formats, neither of which was designed to be perfectly comparable across institutions, and both of which matter a great deal in a narrow window of your life — campus placements, immediate postgraduate admissions, and first-job eligibility — before declining fairly rapidly in significance as your professional record replaces your academic one as the relevant signal.

During that window, know your numbers exactly, know how they convert, know what the cutoffs are for the specific opportunities you're pursuing, and direct your energy accordingly. Above the relevant threshold, stop obsessing — no amount of CGPA maximisation above the cutoff returns meaningfully more than what you'd gain from building real skills, real projects, and a real professional network.

And if you're already past that window and still worrying about a number on a marksheet from three years ago: you're done. It's done its job, for better or worse, and the rest of your career belongs to a completely different set of signals that you have full control over building right now.
